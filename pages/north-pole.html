The North Pole, located in the Arctic Ocean, is the northernmost point on Earth. It’s defined as the point in the Northern Hemisphere where the Earth’s axis of rotation meets its surface. Unlike the South Pole, which is located on a land mass (Antarctica), the North Pole is located in the middle of the Arctic Ocean amid waters that are almost permanently covered with constantly shifting sea ice. This makes it impossible to construct a permanent station at the North Pole, unlike the South Pole. The North Pole is significantly warmer than the South Pole because it lies at sea level in the middle of an ocean (which acts as a reservoir of heat), rather than at altitude on a continental land mass. The sea ice at the North Pole is about 2-3 meters thick. While the North Pole has no land, the sea ice is inhabited by a number of creatures such as polar bears and seals. The North Pole has been a subject of exploration for centuries, with the first successful expedition reaching it in 1909. The North Pole has no time zone, but most explorers and scientists use Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) while there. The sun is continuously above the horizon during the summer and continuously below the horizon during the winter. Interestingly, the North Pole is moving due to the drift of the Earth’s magnetic field. This movement is currently tracked by satellites and is used to update maps and navigation systems. Despite its inhospitable conditions, the North Pole continues to fascinate scientists and adventurers alike.
